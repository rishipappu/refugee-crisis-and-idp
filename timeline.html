Timeline: How Did They Get Here?

2003 - CAR Bush War Begins

Tensions rose and protests broke out after François Bozizé, a high ranking military officer, seized power.

November 2004 - Fighting Begins

Rebels raid a remote village in northern CAR, leading to a full fledged armed conflict between rebels and government forces.

June 21, 2007 - Tentative Peace is Reached

All major rebel groups except one, the CPJP, signed a ceasefire agreement with the government.

August 25, 2012 - CPJP and Bozizé Come to Agreement

The CPJP agrees to lay down arms under certain terms and sign a ceasefire agreement with the government.

December 12, 2012 - Current Conflict Begins

Bozizé accused of not honoring terms of the cease fire agreement, numerous Muslim militias from the north of the country formed a rebel coalition called Séléka

March 24, 2013 - Séléka Takes Control

Séléka forces invaded the CAR capital city, Bangui, and fully wrested control from Bozizé’s government. The group’s leader, Michel Djotodia, was installed as president of the country. For several months afterwards, they engaged in a campaign of murders, rapes, and looting. It was reported that they especially targeted the Christian community.

September 2013 - Séléka is Dissolved

Djotodia announced that Séléka had been dissolved. However, this just lead to the fragmentation of the group, with most members running wild and former generals becoming regional warlords. Members of these split fractions widely identified themselves as ex-Séléka.

December 2013 - Anti-Balaka Retaliation

A large number of local protection forces from Christian areas of the country formed a coalition called “Anti-Balaka”. They intended to take back control of the country and exact revenge on the Muslim community, which they viewed as being responsible for the atrocities commited in Bangui. At this time they commited a number of violent acts. UNICEF and various news outlets reported a variety of atrocities, such as the beheading of children and even the cannibalization of ex-Séléka members.

Current State of Affairs

With the country split in half between ex-Séléka and Anti-Balaka factions, and only further fragmented by the internal power struggles of these groups, the violence has continued. With a population of less than 5 million, it is estimated that over half a million people have been displaced in the CAR. There is still no governmental control in areas outside of Bangui, with most of the country either being dominated by brutal warlords or in a state of complete lawlessness.
