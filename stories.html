Rose Yasambia

Violence is a common occurrence in mainland to the far reaches of the Central African Republic. Rose Yasambia, a late millennial at the age of 30, was one of those affected by the canopy of inhumanity across this landlocked country. She used to live in the Mobayi-Mbongo province, near a local river, where a large majority of the society gathered consistently. One day, out of the blue, Rose heard sporadic gunshots across the current of the water. Many families crossed the river on boats and as Rose looked across the river, from the town Moyabe, she described the militia “setting all the houses on fire”. According to the UN Refugee Agency, “armed groups and the fires have destroyed any hope they had of returning home soon.”

Jean-Pierre Rondosi

Because of this constant violence, many have turned to temporary homes and communities, protected by volunteer agencies. One of these people, Jean-Pierre, who has his own four children family, is one of those who reside in these locations. He and his wife ran from their home after his entire immediate region panicked from gun violence. Jean used to make a living through fishing and harvesting resources from his environment, but without access to land, his job becomes significantly harder. The UNHCR has hosted them along with 793 other people. Efforts like this have helped the community and allowed stories to exit the violence ridden area of the CAR.

Emmanuel Hulen

Emmanuel Hulen was 14 years old when he was forced to leave the Central African Republic due to constant violence. As one of the only possible options for his family to rely on, Emmanuel was given the task of taking low paying jobs to support his younger siblings. According to the UNHCR’s website, “he is [now] an information technology specialist, who has won international awards for his innovations in the field”. People like this are extremely common in CAR; Those who strive to improve living conditions, but are forced to follow a career they don’t want. Though aid and support has been sent, the problem is not and never will be solved by external forces alone. Rather an internal drive, like the one Emmanuel had when he fled, will be critical.
